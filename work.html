My work

My work sits at the uncomfortable but fascinating edge where Energy Storage stops behaving politely. I am a PhD student in Physics working on Thermal Runaway in Lithium-Ion Batteries, a failure process in which a battery transitions from a controlled Electrochemical Device into a rapidly evolving Chemical Reactor.

Once initiated, Thermal Runaway is fast, violent, and unforgiving. Temperature rises within seconds, Gases are released, Materials decompose, and the system quickly outruns any simple Safety Mechanism. Understanding this process in detail is no longer optional. It is a prerequisite for safe Electrification.

At a high level, Thermal Runaway begins when Heat Generation inside a cell exceeds its ability to Dissipate Heat. The trigger can be Mechanical Abuse, Electrical Abuse, or External Heating. Internally, it unfolds as a cascade. The Solid Electrolyte Interphase (SEI) begins to break down. Electrolytes decompose. Electrodes react with each other in ways they were never designed to.

Each step releases additional heat, accelerating the next. The system feeds on itself. By the time flames or venting are visible, the most important Chemistry has already occurred.

Current research has mapped many pieces of this puzzle, but not the full picture. We understand individual Reaction Pathways reasonably well in isolation. We have solid models for Heat Generation under normal operation. We know typical Onset Temperatures for specific Decomposition Mechanisms.

What remains difficult is connecting these pieces into a single, Time-Resolved description of a real Thermal Runaway event. Most measurements are indirect. Most diagnostics integrate over time. Many experiments stop just before things become truly interesting, for understandable Safety reasons.

This is where my work comes in.

I use Laser-Based Diagnostics to observe Thermal Runaway as it unfolds, in real time, without touching the system. Lasers allow Remote, Selective, and extremely Fast interrogation of matter. Instead of measuring Temperature at a single point or collecting post-mortem debris, I probe which Gas Species are released, how their Concentrations evolve, and how Reaction Pathways shift as the event progresses.

At the core of this approach is Spectroscopy. In simple terms, molecules interact with light in very specific ways. Each molecule absorbs or emits light at characteristic Wavelengths that act as Spectral Fingerprints. By tuning a laser to the appropriate Wavelength and measuring how the light changes as it passes through a Gas Plume, it becomes possible to identify which species are present and in what quantities.

Performed fast enough, this yields Time-Resolved Chemistry, not just an outcome.

Different Spectroscopic Techniques emphasize different aspects of the problem. Absorption Spectroscopy tracks toxic and flammable Gases as they form and disappear. Emission Spectroscopy reveals excited species and high-Temperature reaction zones. Laser-Induced Breakdown Spectroscopy (LIBS) probes Elemental Composition when materials violently fragment.

Together, these tools allow reconstruction of the Chemical narrative of Thermal Runaway, frame by frame.

The challenge is not only experimental. Thermal Runaway is not a clean experiment with steady Boundary Conditions. It is a transient, Multi-Physics event involving Heat Transfer, Fluid Dynamics, Chemical Kinetics, and Materials Failure all at once.

A significant part of my work involves extracting meaning from this complexity and translating it into models that are both Physically Grounded and Practically Useful.

The end goal is not merely to describe Thermal Runaway, but to model it as a complete process. From the first Instability to the final Venting Products. From internal reactions to external consequences.

A reliable model enables Prediction. Prediction enables Prevention. And Prevention is where Battery Safety improves, whether through better Materials, smarter Pack Design, Early Detection Systems, or informed Regulation.

In short, I am trying to turn a catastrophic event into a measurable, understandable, and ultimately manageable phenomenon. Batteries will continue to power more of the world. My job is to help ensure they fail less often, and when they do fail, that we understand exactly why.
