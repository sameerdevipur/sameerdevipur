My Work

My work sits at the uncomfortable but fascinating edge where energy storage stops behaving politely. I am a PhD student in Physics working on thermal runaway in lithium-ion batteries, a failure process in which a battery transitions from a controlled electrochemical device into a rapidly evolving chemical reactor.

Once initiated, thermal runaway is fast, violent, and unforgiving. Temperature rises within seconds, gases are released, materials decompose, and the system quickly outruns any simple safety mechanism. Understanding this process in detail is no longer optional. It is a prerequisite for safe electrification.

At a high level, thermal runaway begins when heat generation inside a cell exceeds its ability to dissipate that heat. Internally, it unfolds as a cascade. The solid electrolyte interphase breaks down, electrolytes decompose, and electrodes react in ways they were never designed to.

Each step releases additional heat, accelerating the next. By the time flames or venting are visible, the most important chemistry has already occurred.

This is where my work comes in.

I use laser-based diagnostics to observe thermal runaway as it unfolds, in real time, without touching the system. Lasers allow remote, selective, and extremely fast interrogation of matter, revealing which gas species are released and how reaction pathways evolve.

Together, these tools allow reconstruction of the chemical narrative of thermal runaway, frame by frame. The end goal is prediction, and prediction enables prevention.

Batteries will continue to power more of the world. My work aims to ensure they fail less often and, when they do fail, that we understand exactly why.
