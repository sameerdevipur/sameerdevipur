My work

My work sits at the uncomfortable but fascinating edge where energy storage stops behaving politely. I am a PhD student in physics working on thermal runaway in lithium-ion batteries, a failure process where a battery transitions from a controlled electrochemical device into a rapidly evolving chemical reactor.

Once initiated, thermal runaway is fast, violent, and unforgiving. Temperatures rise within seconds, gases are released, materials decompose, and the system quickly outruns any simple safety mechanism. Understanding this process in detail is not optional anymore. It is a prerequisite for safe electrification.

At a high level, thermal runaway begins when heat generation inside a cell exceeds its ability to dissipate that heat. The trigger can be mechanical abuse, electrical abuse, or external heating. Internally, it is a cascade. The solid electrolyte interphase starts to break down. Electrolytes decompose. Electrodes react with each other in ways they were never designed to.

Current research has mapped many pieces of this puzzle, but not the full picture. We understand individual reactions reasonably well in isolation. What remains difficult is connecting these pieces into a single, time-resolved description of a real thermal runaway event.

This is where my work comes in.

I use laser-based diagnostics to observe thermal runaway as it unfolds, in real time, without touching the system. Lasers allow remote, selective, and extremely fast interrogation of reacting systems.

At the core of this approach is spectroscopy. Molecules interact with light in specific ways. Each species absorbs or emits light at characteristic wavelengths that act as fingerprints. Tracking these interactions enables time-resolved chemistry rather than post-mortem interpretation.

Different spectroscopic techniques emphasize different parts of the problem. Absorption spectroscopy tracks toxic and flammable gases. Emission spectroscopy reveals excited species and high-temperature reaction zones. Laser-induced breakdown spectroscopy probes elemental composition when materials violently fragment.

The challenge is not only experimental. Thermal runaway is a transient, multi-physics event involving heat transfer, fluid dynamics, chemical kinetics, and materials failure all at once. Extracting meaning from this complexity is a central part of my work.

The end goal is not just to describe thermal runaway, but to model it as a complete process. Prediction enables prevention. Prevention is where battery safety improves.

In short, I am trying to turn a catastrophic event into a measurable, understandable, and ultimately manageable phenomenon.
